The Discipline of Rest: A Warrior's Sleep

For warriors · ~6 min read

युक्ताहारविहारस्य युक्तचेष्टस्य कर्मसु।
युक्तस्वप्नावबोधस्य योगो भवति दुःखहा॥ Bhagavad Gītā 6.17 — "For one who is moderate in food and recreation, balanced in action, balanced in sleep and waking — yoga becomes the destroyer of sorrow."

Arjuna is told to fight. He is also told to sleep correctly. Both instructions come from the same teacher, in the same conversation. This is not coincidence. A warrior who cannot rest is a warrior with a short career.

Rest is not the opposite of training

The adaptation you wanted from today's session does not happen during the session. It happens in the hours after, most of them while you are unconscious. The session is the stimulus. Sleep is where the stimulus becomes strength.

Skip the sleep and you have done the hard part — the breathing, the bruising, the decision to show up — and thrown away the easy part. It is the worst trade in athletics.

What the data actually says

Adults need roughly seven to nine hours. Athletes in heavy training generally sit at the upper end. Chronic sleep below six hours is associated with measurable decreases in reaction time, decision quality, and immune function. None of this is mystical. It is boring, repeatable physiology.

Cite this to yourself when the temptation arrives to do "one more round of admin" at midnight. The round is not free. You are borrowing it from tomorrow's session at a punishing interest rate.

A protocol

Anchor the wake time

Pick one wake time and hold it across the week, including weekends. Bedtime drifts; wake time should not. The body calibrates against the wake.

Sundown the screens

Ninety minutes before sleep, the phone goes on a shelf in another room. If this sounds extreme, notice the resistance — it is the same resistance you train past in any other domain.

Eat early, finish hydrating

Last meaningful meal three hours before bed. Last meaningful water one hour before. You cannot sleep deeply while digesting heavily or while your bladder is about to wake you at 3 a.m.

Cool, dark, quiet

Around 18°C / 65°F, blackout darkness, white noise or silence. These are not preferences. They are the conditions under which the nervous system agrees to stand down.

Five minutes of stillness

Before sleep, sit upright for five minutes. Breathe slowly. Do not plan tomorrow. Do not relitigate today. Just breathe. Then lie down. This is the dismount. Warriors who skip the dismount tend to wake at 2 a.m. with their jaw clenched.

The Gītā framing

Krishna does not tell Arjuna to fight harder. He tells him to fight from the right interior state — clear, unattached to outcome, present in the action. That state has a prerequisite, and the prerequisite is rest. A nervous system that has not slept cannot do nishkāma karma ("action without grasping"). It can only do reactive, anxious, fatigued action that wears its label of "effort."

योगस्थः कुरु कर्माणि सङ्गं त्यक्त्वा धनञ्जय। Bhagavad Gītā 2.48 — "Established in yoga, perform your actions, abandoning attachment."

"Established in yoga" is not a metaphor for caffeine. It is the steadiness of a rested, integrated body. Earn it the way you earn anything else worth having: by showing up to it nightly, on a schedule, without negotiating.

One last thing

If you are a fighter, an engineer, a parent, a medic, a builder — anyone whose day-work requires a working nervous system — treat your sleep window as sacred territory. Defend it the way you would defend a teammate. It is, in a literal sense, the teammate that does the repair work while you are gone.
